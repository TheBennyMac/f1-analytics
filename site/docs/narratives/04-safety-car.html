Safety Car Lottery

Does SC timing arbitrarily reshape race results?

The narrative

“The safety car is a lottery — its timing is arbitrary and disproportionately reshuffles the field, benefiting drivers who happened to pit just before deployment and penalising those who just pitted or were running long.”

The hypothesis predicts that races with safety car deployments show materially different position change patterns to clean races — and that more deployments compound the effect.

Method

Distinct SC, VSC, and red flag deployment events are counted per race from FastF1 race control messages. These are then correlated with the overtake index (positions gained ÷ starters) for each race.

Data covers 2022–2025 (92 races).

Findings

Question Finding
SC deployment rate across 2022–2025? High overall — peaked at 73% in 2022, fell to 33% in 2024, rose to 50% in 2025
Do SC races produce more position changes than clean races? Yes — mean overtake index rises from 1.487 (0 SCs) to 1.767 (1 SC)
Does a second SC add further position change activity? Yes — 2+ SCs reaches 2.131, a clear step above 1 SC
Is the “lottery” narrative supported? Yes

Verdict: Supported.

Each additional safety car deployment correlates with a measurable increase in position change activity, and the effect compounds. A race with two or more safety cars produces 43% more position changes per starter than a clean race (2.131 vs 1.487).

The 2022 season — with a 73% SC rate — stands out as particularly intervention-heavy, likely reflecting teams’ unfamiliarity with the new ground effect cars producing more incidents. The declining rate through 2024 may reflect stricter deployment thresholds, faster incident clearance, or simply fewer incidents as teams converged on safer setups.

The data supports the “lottery” framing: SC timing reshuffles the field in ways that are uncorrelated with underlying race pace.
