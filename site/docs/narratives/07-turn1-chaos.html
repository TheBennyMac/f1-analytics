Turn 1 Chaos

Are regulation reset years more incident-prone on the opening lap?

The narrative

“Turn 1 is where races are won and lost — and it’s worse in regulation reset years. Unfamiliar cars, uncertain relative pace, and aggressive positioning combine to produce more first-lap chaos when the regulations change.”

The hypothesis predicts that Year 1 of a new regulation era shows a higher rate of first-lap retirements than settled era years (Year 2+).

Method

A first-lap retirement is defined as a driver who:

  1. Had a valid grid position (started the race)
  2. Did not finish (DNF status)
  3. Completed at most 2 laps — capturing lap 1 incidents plus cars that limped to the pits immediately after

FastF1 assigns finishing positions 1–20 to all drivers including DNFs, so lap count (from lap-by-lap data) is used rather than finish position to identify early retirements.

Data covers 2022–2025 (92 races, all four years of the Ground Effect Era).

Findings

Question Finding
Is the first-lap DNF rate higher in Year 1? Yes — Year 1 (2022) recorded a 2.1% mean rate, more than double any subsequent year
How does Year 1 compare to Years 2–4? ~3.5× higher: 2022 = 2.1%, 2023 = 0.9%, 2024 = 0.6%, 2025 = 0.6%
Is there a convergence trend? Yes — the rate falls monotonically and stabilises by Year 3
Does lap 1 position change activity support the hypothesis? GridPosition not present in the FastF1 laps dataset — metric could not be computed

Verdict: Supported.

First-lap retirements are clearly elevated in the regulation reset year. Year 1 (2022) produced a mean first-lap DNF rate of 2.1% — roughly 3.5 times the rate of Years 2–4 (0.6–0.9%). The rate falls monotonically across the era and stabilises by Year 3, consistent with teams and drivers learning the limits of the new machinery.

The DNS data shows an interesting counter-pattern: Year 1 had the fewest drivers failing to start, suggesting teams prioritised getting cars to the grid in the reset year even at some reliability cost. By Year 2, more conservative decisions about race entry appear in the data.

Implication for 2026: If the pattern holds, the first-lap incident rate in 2026 should be elevated relative to the settled Ground Effect Era years — and should decline by 2027–2028 as teams converge on safer setups. This will be tracked as 2026 data matures.
